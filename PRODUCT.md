## Users

**Primary:** The operator — a creative-finance real estate investor managing their own deal pipeline and portfolio holdings. High-trust, high-context. Opens the app in deal-evaluation mode: checking waterfall math, moving deals through stages, reviewing KPI health. Expects density and speed, not guidance.

**Secondary:** Key principals (KPs) — partners or investors who receive a read-only or scoped view of specific deals and portfolio performance. Less frequent sessions, more likely on mobile, focused on a single deal or a summary view.

Both user types treat this as a private instrument. They've earned the right to it. The interface should feel like something you get access to, not something you sign up for.

## Product Purpose

Portfolio AI is a deal management platform for creative-finance real estate investors. It tracks deals through a pipeline from lead through close, models waterfall cash flows (Morby Method, Seller Finance, Subject-To structures), and surfaces portfolio-level KPIs across active holdings.

Success looks like: an operator can open the app, check their pipeline health, run waterfall math on a deal, and make an acquisition decision — without friction, without help, without noise.

## Brand Personality

**Precise · Private · Institutional**

The emotional register is quiet authority. Not startup confidence; the settled assurance of a Bloomberg terminal or a private bank portal. The interface does not need to sell itself — the numbers do the work. Discretion is a feature.

Voice and tone: direct, minimal, never clever. Labels are facts, not copy. Empty states are brief and actionable. Error messages are precise, never apologetic.

## References

- **Bloomberg Terminal / Refinitiv** — Dense information architecture, data-first hierarchy, institutional gravity. The model for how to make complex financial data navigable without dumbing it down.
- **Traditional private bank / family office portal** — Exclusivity, discretion, heavy typography, restrained color. The feeling that this tool was made for a specific class of operator, not the general market.

## Anti-references

- **Generic SaaS (HubSpot, Monday.com)** — Bubbly, colorful, friendly. Designed for demos and trials. Celebrating onboarding and empty states with mascots and confetti. Nothing about Portfolio AI should suggest it's optimized for acquisition.
- **Consumer fintech (Robinhood, Mint)** — Retail. Gamified. Dopamine loops. Portfolio AI's users are sophisticated operators, not retail investors checking a brokerage account.
- **Enterprise bloat (Salesforce, SAP)** — Dense and utilitarian in the wrong direction: complexity without craft, bureaucratic rather than precise.

## Design Principles

1. **Data above ceremony.** Every UI element earns its place by serving the data. Decoration is a cost, not a feature. When in doubt, remove.

2. **Private intelligence.** This is a tool for an operator who knows what they're doing. Assume context. Skip the onboarding voice, the guided tours, the feature hints. The interface should feel like a Bloomberg terminal: you either know how to use it or you ask.

3. **Operator cadence.** The primary session is fast and intent-driven: check a number, move a deal, run math. Density serves this user. Whitespace is for emphasis, not for filling space between features.

4. **Institutional weight.** Interactions carry gravity. State changes are deliberate. Animations signal meaning — a deal closing, a waterfall updating — not decoration. Nothing bounces or celebrates unless a real milestone is hit.

5. **Principal clarity.** Operator view and KP view are fundamentally different roles. Permission layers are a first-class design concern: what each role sees, how much context they get, and where they're redirected are design decisions, not engineering afterthoughts.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

No specific WCAG requirements at this stage. Standard browser defaults apply. Color contrast for primary data labels should remain readable (4.5:1 for body text as a baseline practice), particularly given the dark theme and the density of financial data.
